**Item 23.** *(Bridge Content: Inversion + Hedging)*

Write 1–2 sentences about a social issue, professional challenge, or global trend that matters to you. Your sentences **must include**:

- One **inversion pattern** (e.g., "Not only did/does... but also...", "Never have I...", "Rarely does...", "Seldom has...")
- One **hedging expression** (e.g., "arguably" / "notwithstanding this" / "it could be argued that" / "to some extent")

*Suggested topics: climate change, workplace inequality, digital privacy, urbanisation, education reform.*

---

**Item 24.** *(Bridge Content: Nominalization + C1 Collocation)*

Write 1–2 sentences about technology, society, or a personal experience or decision. Your sentences **must include**:

- One **nominalization** — using a noun form derived from a verb or adjective (e.g., "the *implementation* of...", "the *deterioration* of...", "a lack of *compliance*", "the *emergence* of...")
- **One** of the following C1-level collocations: `give rise to` / `fall short of` / `cast doubt on` / `exert pressure on` / `in the wake of`

### Research Basis and Methodology

This test was designed using the following research sources:

1. **Cambridge CAE (C1 Advanced) Use of English** — Part 1 (MCQ Cloze: collocations, idioms, confusables), Part 2 (Open Cloze: grammar/structure), Part 3 (Word Formation: productive affixation), Part 4 (Key Word Transformations: lexico-grammar paraphrase including inversion). Stage 3A items mirror CAE Part 4 transformation types.

2. **Cambridge English Vocabulary Profile (EVP, Capel 2010/2012)** — Level assignment is per word *sense*, not per word. C1 introduces 1,622 new senses and headwords beyond B2. Idiom count: B2 = 12 documented; C1 = 39 (3.25× growth). Half of all EVP phrases are C-level items. `notwithstanding` is assigned C1 by Cambridge dictionary; `albeit` is C1/C2 boundary.

3. **Nation & Beglar (2007) / Milton & Alexiou (2009) Vocabulary Size Research** — B2 = ~4,000–5,000 word families (Nation); C1 = ~6,000–8,000 word families. Text coverage: B2 ≈ 95%; C1 approaches 98% (Nation 2006). The 95% → 98% gap requires ~3,000–4,000 additional word families — the functional definition of the B2→C1 jump.

4. **Indonesian EFL Learner Research (ResearchGate, UGM Humaniora, Premise Journal)** — Hardest C1 areas for Indonesian learners: (1) verb+noun collocations — L1 transfer produces "do a mistake," "strong rain," "make a research" instead of "make an error," "heavy rain," "conduct research"; (2) articles in complex anaphoric/generic contexts (absent in Indonesian); (3) near-synonym discrimination — one L1 equivalent for multiple English near-synonyms. Stage 3B Item 24 directly targets collocation precision.

5. **Discourse Marker Research (Huang, Lin & Gráf, John Benjamins; Cambridge Language Research)** — C1 learners shift distribution from conversational DMs (*well, you know*) to formal/textual ones (*notwithstanding, albeit, insofar as*). B2 learners hedge with modals; C1 learners use epistemic distancing phrases ("It would appear that...", "Evidence suggests..."). Item 20 (arguably) and Item 23 (production) target this shift.

**Strongest B2→C1 discriminators selected:**
- **Inversion** (Day 3) — CAE Part 4 tested; B2 users recognise it; C1 users produce it
- **Formal subjunctive** (Day 12) — "that he *be* present" (no -s); missing from B2 intuition; systematic at C1
- **Cleft sentences** (Day 13) — "What surprised everyone was..." — C1 emphasis device
- **"arguably"** (Day 18) — distincts from *presumably/apparently/evidently*; epistemic hedge at C1
- **"notwithstanding"** (Day 19) — Cambridge-verified C1 level; Indonesian learners systematically underuse formal connectors (UGM research)
- **Nominalization + abstract collocation** (Stage 3B) — automatic in formal writing at C1; Indonesian learners default to verb-based sentences under production pressure
