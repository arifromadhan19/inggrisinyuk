## PART A — In-App Vocabulary Assessment

> **Platform:** Web app — runs entirely in-app, no ChatGPT, no external AI.
> **Format:** 15 multiple-choice questions (A/B/C/D), one correct answer each.
> **Timer:** None — attempt at own pace.
> **Score:** Calculated automatically. Displayed immediately after submission.

### The 15 Questions

---

**Q1 — Connotation precision**
Which word describes someone who speaks directly and honestly in a professional context and carries a **positive** connotation?

A) blunt  
B) frank  
C) curt  
D) brusque

---

**Q2 — Idiom collocation precision**
Which sentence uses the idiom *strike a chord* correctly?

A) The speech struck a chord on the audience.  
B) His announcement struck a chord with investors across the region.  
C) She struck the chord and the room resonated.  
D) That idea strikes a good chord in me.

---

**Q3 — Litotes in context**
Which sentence uses litotes correctly AND with genuine rhetorical effect?

A) The report wasn't terrible.  
B) The results were not bad at all.  
C) Negotiating that ceasefire was no mean feat.  
D) It wasn't not well-organised.

---

**Q4 — Register appropriateness**
You are writing a formal statement about an incident where someone was harmed due to poor planning. Which phrasing is MOST appropriate?

A) They got killed due to bad planning.  
B) They lost their lives as a consequence of inadequate preparation.  
C) They died because terrible decisions were made.  
D) They were done in by sheer incompetence.

---

**Q5 — Register mismatch detection**
Identify the word that does NOT match the register of this formal speech opening:
*"Ladies and gentlemen, we gather today to ________ the remarkable legacy of a scientist who changed the field forever."*

A) commemorate  
B) celebrate  
C) mark  
D) big up

---

**Q6 — Idiom manipulation vs. standard use**
Which sentence demonstrates a deliberate idiom **manipulation** — not just standard use?

A) Don't count your chickens before they hatch.  
B) Don't count your chickens before they hatch — or, in this organisation, before they unionize.  
C) You shouldn't count your chickens.  
D) Count your chickens carefully before making promises.

---

**Q7 — Near-synonym neutrality**
A journalist is writing about a politician who changed their position after public criticism. Which word choice is the MOST NEUTRAL?

A) flip-flopped  
B) reversed their position  
C) capitulated  
D) caved

---

**Q8 — Rhetorical device identification**
Identify the primary rhetorical device in this sentence:
*"We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets..."*

A) Hyperbole  
B) Litotes  
C) Anaphora  
D) Antithesis

---

**Q9 — Rhetorical fronting vs. grammatical inversion**
Which sentence uses inversion as a **deliberate C2 rhetorical craft choice** — for narrative or persuasive effect — rather than as a formal grammatical structure (C1 inverted conditional or B2 negative adverbial)?

A) Had the policy been reversed, the outcome would have been different.  
B) Were it not for her intervention, the project would have failed.  
C) Such was the impact of his closing argument that the jury deliberated for less than an hour.  
D) Not only did she finish first, but she broke the record.

---

**Q10 — Collocation precision**
Choose the word that correctly completes the natural English collocation:
*"The documentary ________ a powerful argument for environmental policy reform."*

A) did  
B) made  
C) performed  
D) conducted

---

**Q11 — Discourse marker register**
Which discourse marker best fits the **native conversational register** in this context?
*"I know the plan isn't perfect — ________, it's the best option we have right now."*

A) notwithstanding  
B) insofar as  
C) for what it's worth  
D) by virtue of

---

**Q12 — Anaphora recognition**
Which sentence uses anaphora (deliberate repetition of a word/phrase at the beginning of successive clauses for rhetorical effect)?

A) I came, I saw, I conquered.  
B) She worked tirelessly, diligently, and — in the end — thanklessly.  
C) The results were surprising; the implications were vast.  
D) Having listened, having learned, and having changed, we moved forward.

---

**Q13 — Modal nuance for moral stance**
Which sentence uses a modal verb to express a subtle **moral judgement** — implying that a different, better action was possible but not taken?

A) She couldn't attend the meeting.  
B) She might have said something. She didn't.  
C) She must have been aware of the issue.  
D) She should have a degree in this field.

---

**Q14 — Concession structure**
Which sentence uses a concession correctly — acknowledging a counterpoint before reasserting a stronger claim?

A) Although the evidence is mixed, I think it's probably true.  
B) Granted, there are valid concerns about implementation — but the core argument remains sound.  
C) Despite having reservations, the decision was made.  
D) However, the results were not what we expected.

---

**Q15 — Rhetorical craft synthesis**
Which sentence demonstrates the most deliberate **C2 rhetorical craft** — using multiple devices working together, rather than just correct grammar?

A) The policy was controversial and had many supporters and critics.  
B) Was the policy perfect? Far from it. Did it change things? Unequivocally.  
C) Despite being controversial, the policy had considerable support.  
D) The policy, while not without flaws, had significant backing.

### Answer Key (Hardcoded in App)

| Q | Answer | Reasoning (for developer reference / Kak Ara feedback text) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | B | *Frank* = honest + open, positive in professional contexts. *Blunt* is neutral; *curt* and *brusque* imply rudeness. |
| 2 | B | *Strike a chord with someone* — correct collocation. A has wrong preposition (*on*); C/D misuse the idiom entirely. |
| 3 | C | *No mean feat* is idiomatic litotes with genuine rhetorical force. A/B are weak negatives without rhetorical intent; D is a double negative error. |
| 4 | B | Passive/impersonal + formal register throughout. A is casual; C is direct but insufficient formality; D uses slang (*done in*). |
| 5 | D | *Big up* is colloquial/slang — entirely wrong register for a formal commemorative speech. |
| 6 | B | Deliberate **extension** of the idiom with ironic meaning change. A is standard use; C/D abandon the idiom structure. |
| 7 | B | *Reversed their position* is neutral. *Flip-flopped* = mocking; *capitulated* = implies surrender; *caved* = implies weakness. |
| 8 | C | **Anaphora**: deliberate repetition of *we shall fight* at the start of each clause for rhythmic, persuasive effect. |
| 9 | C | *Such was... that* = C2 rhetorical fronting for narrative effect. A and B = C1 inverted conditionals (formal "if" substitute). D = B2 negative adverbial inversion. |
| 10 | B | *Make an argument* is the correct English collocation. *Do/perform/conduct an argument* are not idiomatic. |
| 11 | C | *For what it's worth* = native conversational discourse marker that acknowledges limitation while asserting value. A/B/D are formal/academic markers, wrong register for this context. |
| 12 | A | *I came, I saw, I conquered* — classic **anaphora** (repetition of "I") + rhetorical triad. B is parallel list; C is two balanced clauses; D is participial parallelism. |
| 13 | B | *Might have said something. She didn't.* — modal past implies moral awareness: she had the option and chose not to act. A = inability; C = logical deduction; D = professional requirement. |
| 14 | B | *Granted... but* = the complete concession structure: full acknowledgement of counterpoint + explicit rebuttal. A lacks a clear rebuttal; C has no reasserted claim; D has no prior claim to concede against. |
| 15 | B | Uses **four** C2 devices together: rhetorical questions + antithesis + litotes (*far from it*) + emphatic adverb (*unequivocally*). A is grammatically correct but rhetorically flat. C/D use hedging without rhetorical intent. |

### Part B2 — Speaking: 3 Craft-Demanding Questions

**Instructions (full English):** No preparation time. These questions test whether the craft from the writing carries over under real-time pressure.

**Q1 — Rhetorical craft under pressure (the core differentiator question):**
> "Without preparation: give me the closing lines of a eulogy for someone you admired — real or invented. Thirty seconds to a minute. Focus on what you choose to say, and how you say it."

*What this checks:* whether Day 1 fronting, Day 5 parallelism/anaphora, Day 2 understatement, and Day 13 ceremonial register appear automatically in unscripted speech — not just in planned writing.

**Q2 — Idiom manipulation on the spot:**
> "Pick any idiom you know well — your choice. Manipulate it right now: subvert it, extend it, or take it literally to make a point. Then in one sentence, tell me why that manipulation works."

*What this checks:* Day 3's idiom manipulation under real-time pressure + metalinguistic awareness — the ability to notice and articulate what makes a rhetorical choice effective.

**Q3 — Near-synonym + connotation under real-time pressure:**
> "What's the difference between being *persistent* and being *stubborn*? And — without thinking about it too long — which one do you think you are?"

*What this checks:* Day 4's near-synonym connotation precision deployed spontaneously. The personal question tests whether the learner can make a confident, precise self-characterisation in English under no-prep conditions.

## Research Basis and Methodology

1. **Why Part A (MCQ) is fully hardcodeable.** Cambridge English Proficiency (CPE) Part 1 — Multiple-Choice Cloze, 8 items — focuses on vocabulary precision: idioms, collocations, near-synonyms, register. The 15-item Part A directly models this diagnostic logic. Each question isolates a distinct C2 competency; all questions have a single, unambiguous correct answer with clear differentiating logic (documented in the Answer Key above). This makes Part A fully deterministic — no AI evaluation needed, suitable for in-app implementation with hardcoded logic.

2. **Why Part B (Essay + Speaking) still requires ChatGPT.** Rhetorical craft is not binary — there is no correct answer to "did this fronting structure land well?" or "was this idiom manipulation deliberate or accidental?" These are judgements that require reading the full context of what a learner wrote and said, understanding intent, and comparing quality against a native-like standard. They cannot be reduced to string-matching or rule-based scoring. Part B is therefore the component that genuinely requires AI, and the ChatGPT URL mechanism (same as Day 1–30) is the appropriate delivery vehicle.

3. **Why the two-part structure resolves the PRD vs. Vocab.md conflict.** `PRD_USER.md` §4.2 requires 10–15 MCQ with a numeric score — Part A satisfies this exactly (15 questions, automatic score). `Vocab.md` §H requires Writing + Speaking for craft assessment — Part B satisfies this exactly.
