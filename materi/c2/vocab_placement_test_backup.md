# C2 Mastery Confirmation — Vocabulary Module (Day 31)

**Level:** C2 (Mastery / Proficient) | **Module:** Vocabulary | **AI Tutor:** Kak Ara

---

## Overview

This is the Day 31 assessment that closes the C2 Vocabulary track and confirms Mastery. Unlike the equivalent assessment at every level from A1 to C1, this is not a "placement test for the next level" — C2 is the highest CEFR level, so the question being asked here is not *"is this learner ready for C3?"* (there is no C3) but *"is this learner's Mastery authentic — the kind that survives real compositional pressure and real-time cognitive load?"*

The format is built directly from the **format baku** specified in `Materi/C2/Vocab.md` (§H, Final Capstone Assessment): **Vocabulary Precision Check (8 items) + Writing (one free-form essay, 200–250 words, choice of three topics) + Speaking (three reflective questions demanding craft rather than just correct answers)**. The Vocabulary Precision Check draws on Cambridge English Proficiency (CPE) assessment design: at C2, precision in near-synonym discrimination, connotation, and register is as diagnostic as extended production, and an 8-item warm-up surfaces this precision quickly before the essay begins.

**Why extended production and not multiple choice as the core format:** at A1–C1, discrete-item assessments measure *knowledge* — does the learner know these words, structures, this register. By the end of C2, knowledge is assumed. What remains is whether that knowledge survives: (a) real compositional pressure in the essay, where craft choices must be deliberate rather than accidental, and (b) real-time cognitive load in speaking, where devices from the planned essay must also appear in unscripted answers. These cannot be measured by MCQ alone.

> **Note on PRD consistency:** `PRD_USER.md` §4.2 states a generic Day 31 Vocabulary format (10–15 MCQ-style items, numeric score) applicable by default across all six levels. `Materi/C2/Vocab.md` §H overrides this explicitly for C2 with its own format baku — consistent with the same file's documented claim that C2 marks a shift from accuracy-testing to craft and cultural fluency. This test follows the **C2-specific override**. If unintentional, `PRD_USER.md` §4.2 should be updated to document the C1 and C2 exceptions.

## What This Test Measures

| Dimension | What it checks | Why it matters at C2 Mastery |
|---|---|---|
| Vocabulary precision | Whether near-synonyms are chosen deliberately for connotation, not interchangeably | At C2, precision is the differentiator — two learners may both "know" *contend* and *assert*, but only one knows which to reach for in a given rhetorical moment |
| Rhetorical craft in writing | Whether C2 devices (fronting, litotes, idiom manipulation, parallelism) are deployed deliberately for effect — or correctly but mechanically | The C1→C2 shift is not "more grammar" but "deliberate grammar as rhetorical choice" — `Vocab.md` documents this as the defining feature of the C2 curriculum |
| Register control | Whether the essay maintains a consistent, purposeful register — and shifts deliberately when the topic calls for it | C2 adds formal↔colloquial↔ironic↔diplomatic range to C1's formal↔natural distinction; consistent register across a 200–250 word essay under no time pressure is the minimum expected |
| Craft under pressure | Whether rhetorical devices that appeared in the planned essay also appear — in some form — in unscripted speaking answers | The gap between writing (time to plan) and speaking (no plan) is the most reliable diagnostic of whether C2 vocabulary is genuinely available or only accessible with preparation |
| Near-synonym discrimination | Whether the test-taker can identify — and explain — the difference between near-synonyms in context: connotation, register, emotional weight | Cambridge English Proficiency specifically tests this at C2 — named explicitly in `Vocab.md`'s own "Perbedaan C1 vs C2" table: "distinguish between near-synonyms and understand subtle differences in meaning" |

## Part 0 — Vocabulary Precision Check

**Instructions (shown to the test-taker, full English):**

> Answer all 8 questions before the writing task. No preparation time — these are read-once, answer-once. Select A, B, C, or D for each.

**Q1 — Connotation precision**
Which word describes someone who speaks directly and honestly in a professional context, and carries a **positive** connotation?

A) blunt — B) frank — C) curt — D) brusque

**Q2 — Idiom collocation precision**
Which sentence uses the idiom *strike a chord* correctly?

A) The speech struck a chord on the audience.
B) His announcement struck a chord with investors across the region.
C) She struck the chord and the room resonated.
D) That idea strikes a good chord in me.

**Q3 — Litotes in context**
Which sentence uses litotes correctly AND with genuine rhetorical effect?

A) The report wasn't terrible.
B) The results were not bad at all.
C) Negotiating that ceasefire was no mean feat.
D) It wasn't not well-organised.

**Q4 — Register appropriateness**
You are writing a formal statement about an incident where someone was harmed due to poor planning. Which phrasing is MOST appropriate?

A) They got killed due to bad planning.
B) They lost their lives as a consequence of inadequate preparation.
C) They died because terrible decisions were made.
D) They were done in by sheer incompetence.

**Q5 — Register mismatch detection**
Identify the word that does NOT match the register of this formal speech opening:
*"Ladies and gentlemen, we gather today to ________ the remarkable legacy of a scientist who changed the field forever."*

A) commemorate — B) celebrate — C) mark — D) big up

**Q6 — Idiom manipulation vs. standard use**
Which sentence demonstrates a deliberate idiom **manipulation** (subversion, extension, or literalization) — not just standard use?

A) Don't count your chickens before they hatch.
B) Don't count your chickens before they hatch — or, in this organisation, before they unionize.
C) You shouldn't count your chickens.
D) Count your chickens carefully before making promises.

**Q7 — Near-synonym neutrality**
A journalist is writing about a politician who changed their position after public criticism. Which word choice is the MOST NEUTRAL (no editorial bias)?

A) flip-flopped — B) reversed their position — C) capitulated — D) caved

**Q8 — Rhetorical device identification**
Identify the primary rhetorical device in this sentence:
*"We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets..."*

A) Hyperbole — B) Litotes — C) Anaphora — D) Antithesis

### Answer Key (Kak Ara only — do not reveal during the test)

| Q | Answer | Why |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | B | *Frank* = honest + open, positive in professional contexts; *blunt* is neutral; *curt* and *brusque* imply rudeness |
| 2 | B | *Strike a chord with someone* is the correct collocation; A has wrong preposition; C/D misuse the idiom entirely |
| 3 | C | *No mean feat* is idiomatic litotes with clear rhetorical force; A/B are weak negatives without rhetorical intent; D is a double negative error |
| 4 | B | Passive/impersonal + formal register; A is casual; C is direct but insufficiently formal; D uses slang (*done in*) |
| 5 | D | *Big up* is colloquial/slang — entirely wrong register for formal ceremonial speech |
| 6 | B | Deliberate extension of the idiom with ironic meaning change; A is standard use; C/D abandon the idiom structure |
| 7 | B | *Reversed their position* is neutral; *flip-flopped* is mocking; *capitulated* implies defeat; *caved* implies weakness |
| 8 | C | Anaphora: deliberate repetition of *we shall fight* at the start of each clause for rhythmic and persuasive effect |

**Scoring Part 0:** Kak Ara notes results per question and uses them to calibrate essay feedback — particularly whether near-synonym and register issues in the essay were anticipated by Q1/Q4/Q5/Q7 results. Part 0 does not produce a standalone score; it informs the overall band assessment.

## Part 2 — Speaking: 3 Craft-Demanding Questions

**Instructions (full English):** No preparation time. These questions do not test whether you can answer correctly — they test whether the craft you showed in the writing carries over when you have no time to plan.

**Q1 — Rhetorical craft under pressure (the core differentiator question):**
> "Without preparation: give me the closing lines of a eulogy for someone you admired — real or invented. Thirty seconds to a minute. Focus on what you choose to say, and how you say it."

*What this checks:* whether Day 1 fronting, Day 5 parallelism/anaphora, Day 2 understatement, and Day 13 ceremonial register appear automatically in unscripted high-stakes speech. The eulogy format is deliberate: ceremonial register is where rhetorical craft either shows up spontaneously or doesn't at all. This is the single most direct test of whether C2 Mastery is available under pressure.

**Q2 — Idiom manipulation on the spot:**
> "Pick any idiom you know well — your choice. Manipulate it right now: subvert it, extend it, or take it literally to make a point. Then in one sentence, tell me why that particular manipulation works."

*What this checks:* Day 3's idiom manipulation under real-time pressure, plus the metalinguistic awareness that separates Mastery from high-level fluency — the ability to notice what makes a rhetorical choice effective, not just produce it. Kak Ara watches specifically for whether the manipulation genuinely "lands" or whether it produces an error that looks like an attempt at humour.

**Q3 — Near-synonym + connotation under real-time pressure:**
> "What's the difference between being *persistent* and being *stubborn*? And — without thinking about it too long — which one do you think you are?"

*What this checks:* Day 4's near-synonym connotation precision deployed spontaneously. The second half ("which one are you?") is deliberately personal — it tests whether the learner can make a confident, precise self-characterisation in English, which requires both vocabulary accuracy and tonal control. Kak Ara notes if the answer hedges unnecessarily, or — the positive signal — commits to a position with wit and precision.

## Scoring & Interpretation

This assessment does not produce a percentage score. `Vocab.md` §H specifies qualitative criteria because what's being measured at C2 is *how* language is used under creative and real-time pressure, not how many items were answered correctly.

| Band | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Mastery Confirmed** | The essay demonstrates craft across multiple rhetorical dimensions — devices appear because the argument called for them, not because the checklist required them. Register is consistent and purposeful; any shifts are deliberate. Part 0 shows reliable near-synonym and connotation control (6–8/8 correct with no pattern errors). Speaking maintains the same rhetorical quality as the writing — devices from the essay also appear, in some form, in unscripted answers. Q1 (eulogy) shows genuine ceremonial instinct; Q2 (idiom manipulation) lands as wit, not error; Q3 (near-synonym) produces a precise, confident, specific self-characterisation. |
| **Mastery in Progress** | The essay shows strong command of one or two rhetorical dimensions but loses control in others — devices may be used correctly but not yet placed where they'd have the most effect. Register is mostly consistent with occasional unintentional slips. Part 0 shows precision in some categories but not others. Speaking is fluent but falls back on safer, less crafted language — the rhetorical quality of the writing does not fully carry over under real-time pressure. Q2 produces a technically correct manipulation that doesn't quite land; Q3 is accurate but hedges rather than commits. *(Name the specific device(s) or Days worth reinforcing before retaking — e.g. Day 4 for near-synonym precision, Day 12 for register-shifting fluency.)* |
| **Return to C2 Curriculum** | Grammatical accuracy is largely intact but rhetorical control is inconsistent — devices are attempted tentatively or without clear effect. Part 0 has multiple near-synonym or register misidentifications (4 or more errors, especially in Q1/Q4/Q5/Q7). Speaking falls back significantly on simpler patterns; the essay's rhetorical choices do not reappear in speaking. Q1 defaults to listing facts rather than shaping a moment; Q2 produces standard idiom use rather than manipulation; Q3 cannot maintain a clear near-synonym distinction under real-time pressure. *(Name specific Days to revisit: Day 1 for rhetorical fronting, Day 2 for litotes, Day 3 for idiom manipulation, Day 4 for connotation precision, Day 12 for register-shifting fluency.)* |

Kak Ara's feedback must always be **specific** — naming the actual rhetorical moments, device choices, and vocabulary from this test-taker's own essay and speaking answers. A generic verdict is not an acceptable outcome of this assessment.

## Research Basis and Methodology

1. **Why a Vocabulary Precision Check (Part 0) is added at C2 specifically.** Cambridge English Proficiency (CPE) — the industry standard for C2 assessment — dedicates a full component to vocabulary precision: Part 1 (Multiple-Choice Cloze, 8 items) focuses specifically on idioms, collocations, fixed phrases, and near-synonym choice in context. The diagnostic logic is that at C2, precision in word choice is as important as quantity — "accuracy and appropriacy, not just quantity" (Cambridge C2 Proficiency Handbook for Teachers, 2023). The 8-item Part 0 in this assessment is modeled on CPE Part 1: each question isolates one distinct C2 competency (connotation, idiom collocation, litotes, register, idiom manipulation, near-synonym neutrality, rhetorical device identification) rather than testing general vocabulary knowledge.

2. **Why extended production — not MCQ — remains the primary format.** CPE allocates substantial weight to extended writing and speaking precisely because rhetorical craft in a free-form essay (planned, time to construct) paired against unscripted speaking (no plan) is the most diagnostic format at C2 level (CPE exam format documentation). `Vocab.md` §H specifies Writing + Speaking — not 10–15 MCQ items — for the same reason. The gap between essay performance and speaking performance is itself a diagnostic signal: a wide gap indicates craft that is only available with preparation, not yet Mastery.

3. **What C2 specifically adds over C1.** `Materi/C2/Vocab.md` explicitly documents the C1→C2 shift: not "more grammar" but "grammar as deliberate rhetorical choice." The five core rhetorical devices in Days 1–5 (Rhetorical Fronting, Litotes, Idiom Manipulation, Near-Synonym Precision, Parallelism/Anaphora) are each contrasted against their C1 equivalents in the file — fronting at C2 is not "more advanced inversion" but "craft choice for dramatic effect, used by speechwriters and novelists." This test's essay rubric and speaking questions are designed to detect precisely this shift.

4. **Near-synonym discrimination as the defining C2 vocabulary skill.** Both Cambridge English Proficiency research and the English Vocabulary Profile (Cambridge Core) identify near-synonym discrimination as the primary C2 vocabulary differentiator — the EVP documents that C2 adds not primarily *more words* but *deeper knowledge per word*: connotation, register, and collocation precision across words a C1 learner treats as interchangeable (*argue* vs. *contend* vs. *assert*; *frugal* vs. *thrifty* vs. *miserly*; *blunt* vs. *frank* vs. *curt* vs. *brusque*). Part 0 Q1, Q4, Q5, and Q7 directly test this; the essay rubric weights it via the "Nuanced Near-Synonym Choice" device row.

5. **Idiom manipulation as a uniquely C2 competency.** The English Vocabulary Profile documents approximately 39 idioms at C1 and 196 at C2 — a five-fold increase — but the more important finding is that C2 introduces *idiom manipulation* (subversion, extension, literalization), which is meaningless at lower levels because it requires complete mastery of the original idiom to bend it convincingly. This is the direct basis for Part 0 Q6 and Speaking Q2, and is explicitly documented in `Vocab.md` Day 3's device contrast table: "C1 uses idioms per standard meaning; C2 manipulates known idioms for comic/ironic craft."

6. **Why this assessment redirects to English For Professionals, not a "next CEFR level."** Post-C2, the CEFR framework offers no further levels. `Vocab.md` §H specifies that the Day 31 capstone redirects learners to English For Professionals (`PRD_USER.md` §4.8) rather than to a notional "C3." This is pedagogically coherent: domain-specific professional fluency (legal English, corporate communication, academic writing) is the natural continuation of general Mastery — not more of the same skill, but the application of existing skills to specialised contexts. The "Mastery Confirmed" scoring band therefore closes with a specific referral to the English For Professionals module.
