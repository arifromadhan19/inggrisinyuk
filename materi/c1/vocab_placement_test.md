## 📋 OVERVIEW

**Goal:** Measure whether you've mastered enough C1 vocabulary, grammar, and register control to progress to Level C2 (Mastery).

**Format:** 4 stages (8 + 8 + 6 + 4 = **26 points total**)

| Stage | Type | Items | Points |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stage 1 | Multiple Choice — Word Recognition (Core C1, Day 1–21) | 8 | 8 |
| Stage 2 | Gap Fill — Word Bank (C1 Bridge/Philosophical, Day 10–29) | 8 | 8 |
| Stage 3A | Multiple Choice — Grammar & Connotation Bridge C1→C2 | 6 | 6 |
| Stage 3B | Mini Production — Write Your Own Sentences | 2 | 4 |
| **Total** | | **24 items + 2 production** | **26** |

**Results:**
- ✅ **21–26 points (80%+):** Ready for C2
- ⚠️ **16–20 points (60–79%):** Almost ready — strengthen specific Days first
- 🔄 **0–15 points (<60%):** Consolidate C1 core before attempting C2

> **Note on format:** `Materi/C1/Vocab.md` §H (line 340) describes Day 31 as an essay + speaking assessment. This implementation instead uses the same coded, deterministic MCQ/gap-fill/production format as every other level (A1–B2), because the Vocabulary Placement Test is built as a static, app-coded quiz rather than a live AI conversation — open-ended essay/speaking grading cannot be deterministically coded. This also resolves the PRD tension noted in this file's earlier draft: the format below now matches PRD_USER.md §4.2's generic Day 31 baseline (10–15+ items, MCQ/gap-fill/error-ID, numeric score) exactly, so no PRD exception is needed. The essay + speaking format remains valuable as a *qualitative* supplementary check — it could be offered separately as an optional "AI Coach deep-dive" conversation with Kak Ara for learners who want register-level feedback before a final C2 enrolment decision, but the graded, codeable placement test itself follows the pattern below.

### Language Policy Note (PRD 5.5 — C1)

At C1, English is the dominant language for instruction and feedback — Full English, with **no Indonesian fallback at all** (PRD 5.5: Full English / Full English / "Tidak ada terjemahan" / ~5% opsional, istilah sangat teknis saja).

**For this Placement Test:** every instruction, sentence, and word bank below is presented in Full English with zero translation, including the word banks and grammar items — reading and producing English without scaffolding is itself part of what C1→C2 readiness means. If you ask for a translation, Kak Ara's scripted response is:

> "At this level there's no Indonesian fallback — tell me which part isn't landing, and I'll rephrase it in simpler English instead."

## STAGE 3A — Multiple Choice: Grammar & Connotation Bridge C1 → C2
**(6 items × 1 point = 6 points)**

> Choose the grammatically correct and contextually most precise answer. Sentences in this stage are intentionally not translated. Cambridge's own CAE (C1) vs. CPE (C2) exam research shows the C1→C2 boundary is carried mainly by *depth and subtlety within the same grammar categories*, not by entirely new categories — so most items below test fine discrimination within structures you already know, not new ones. Item 22 is the one exception: it previews a C2-only device. Don't worry if it's unfamiliar — that's the point of asking it here.

---

**Item 17.** *(Fronting for Emphasis — Day 9 Grammar)*

The committee would only reconsider the proposal if new evidence emerged.

→ Rewrite using fronting for emphasis. Which is correct and most natural?

a) "Only if new evidence emerged would the committee reconsider the proposal."  
b) "Only if new evidence emerged the committee would reconsider the proposal."  
c) "Only if new evidence would emerge, the committee reconsider the proposal."  
d) "New evidence only if emerged would the committee reconsider the proposal."  

---

**Item 18.** *(Academic Discourse Markers — Day 11 Grammar)*

The defendant's account cannot be considered reliable, _____ it directly contradicts the timeline established by phone records.

a) insofar as  
b) by virtue of  
c) notwithstanding  
d) albeit  

---

**Item 19.** *(Epistemic Modals — Day 6 Grammar)*

d) ought not to happen

## STAGE 3B — Mini Production: Write Your Own Sentences
**(2 items × 2 points = 4 points)**

> Write 1–2 sentences for each item. There is no single correct answer — what is assessed is whether you use the required expressions correctly and naturally.

---

**Item 23.** *(Bridge Content: Fronting/Inversion + Epistemic Modal)*

Write 1–2 sentences about a decision, risk, or prediction that matters to you. Your sentences **must include**:

- One **fronting/inversion pattern** (e.g., "Were... to...", "Had it not been for...", "Only by...", "Never before...", "Such was/were... that...")
- One **epistemic modal** (e.g., "might well" / "could conceivably" / "is bound to" / "could scarcely")

---

**Item 24.** *(Bridge Content: Nominalisation + Academic Discourse Marker)*

Write 1–2 sentences about an institution, a news story, or a personal experience involving trust or doubt. Your sentences **must include**:

- One **nominalisation** — a noun formed from a verb or adjective (e.g., "the *erosion* of...", "the *proliferation* of...", "*accountability*", "*consolidation*")
- **One** of the following discourse markers: `insofar as` / `notwithstanding the fact that` / `by virtue of` / `by and large`

## RESEARCH BASIS AND METHODOLOGY

This test was designed using the following research sources:

1. **Cambridge CAE (C1 Advanced) vs. CPE (C2 Proficiency) Use of English** — [C1 Advanced Handbook](https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/Images/167804-c1-advanced-handbook.pdf), [C2 Proficiency Handbook](https://www.cambridgeenglish.org/Images/168194-c2-proficiency-teachers-handbook.pdf). Cambridge's own task-focus wording for Part 1 (Multiple-Choice Cloze: idioms, collocations, fixed phrases, semantic precision) is **identical at both levels** — difficulty is carried by item/text difficulty and frequency, not a documented new category. This directly justifies this test's Stage 1/3A design: deeper connotation/near-synonym discrimination within familiar categories, not invented new categories. Cambridge's Writing scale also names the clearest qualitative differentiator: C1 = lexis used "appropriately", errors "do not impede communication"; C2 = lexis used "effectively and precisely", errors occur only "as slips" in low-frequency words. C2 additionally names "abstract topics" and "colloquial references/asides/cultural allusions" as C2-only constructs.

2. **Cambridge English Vocabulary Profile (EVP)** — Capel, A. (2012), "Completing the English Vocabulary Profile: C1 and C2 vocabulary," *English Profile Journal* 3(1), [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/english-profile-journal/article/completing-the-english-vocabulary-profile-c1-and-c2-vocabulary/418955FC7ED2455E98A499BC40C2C816). Confirmed via direct primary-source quote: "there are 12 idioms at B2, 39 at C1 and 196 at C2" — idiom density roughly quintuples from C1 to C2, far outpacing general vocabulary growth. EVP's actual precision mechanism is grading individual word *senses* (e.g. "short" → "in short" at C1 → "fall short of" at C2), which is the model for this test's within-Day near-synonym items (Stage 1 Item 5, Stage 3A Item 21).

3. **Vocabulary size research** — Milton & Alexiou (2009), CEFR-calibrated X-Lex data: C1 ≈ 3,750–4,500 words, C2 ≈ 4,500–5,000 words (best read as a conservative floor, since X-Lex has a 5,000-word ceiling). Nation (2006), *Canadian Modern Language Review* 63(1): 8,000–9,000 word families needed for 98% text coverage — a different instrument measuring unassisted reading rather than exam-level descriptors, not a contradiction. Schmitt, Jiang & Grabe (2011), *Modern Language Journal* 95(1), found coverage-to-comprehension is a **linear relationship with no sharp threshold** — any specific 98%/99% cutoff should be treated as a design heuristic, not an established fact.

4. **C1→C2 discriminators in SLA research** — The CEFR Companion Volume's vocabulary-range descriptor explicitly assigns "shows awareness of connotative levels of meaning" to C2 only, absent from C1's descriptor — the single most load-bearing official discriminator, and the direct justification for this test's connotation-precision items. Yevchuk (2022), *Taikomoji Kalbotyra*, found C1 learners' near-synonym choices differ statistically from native speakers even at this level (n=45 vs. n=58). Carlsen (EuroSLA Monograph) found additive connectives reach ceiling by B2/C1, while adversative and causal connectives show much higher error rates even at C1 — the direct basis for Stage 3A Item 20 ("notwithstanding"). Bromberek-Dyzman et al. (2023), *Language and Speech*, found L2 learners reliably decode critical irony better than praising irony even near-natively — noted here as a testable asymmetry, though not yet used in this version of the test.

5. **The automaticity/naturalness gap — the most important limitation of this test format.** The CEFR Companion Volume's own wording encodes this: C1 learners "select an appropriate formulation... without having to restrict what they want to say" (implies active search); C2 learners show "no signs of having to restrict" (implies effortless, automatic retrieval). Takizawa, Saito et al. (2025), *Applied Linguistics* (n=210), found automatized vocabulary knowledge (speed/stability of retrieval) predicts L2 speaking fluency far better than declarative knowledge measured by untimed multiple-choice — which showed little to no effect. **This means an untimed, coded MCQ test — exactly the format required here — is, by the research, the weakest possible instrument for measuring the real C1→C2 boundary.** This test should be read as confirming *explicit/declarative* C1→C2 readiness, not *automatic/spontaneous* readiness. If the app's quiz engine can support even a soft per-stage timer (e.g. 90 seconds for Stage 1, 60 seconds for Stage 3A), that would meaningfully sharpen this test's diagnostic power at this specific boundary without requiring a live AI conversation.

**Strongest C1→C2 discriminators selected for this test:**
- **Within-Day near-synonym precision** (Stage 1 Item 5; Stage 3A Item 21) — directly targets the EVP sense-splitting mechanism and the CEFR's connotation-awareness descriptor
- **Adversative/causal connector precision** ("notwithstanding", Stage 3A Item 20) — Carlsen's strongest documented C1-level error source
- **Epistemic modal strength discrimination** ("might well" vs. "is bound to", Item 19) — tests whether degree of certainty, not just grammatical form, is controlled
- **Litotes recognition** (Item 22) — a deliberately light-touch forward probe for C2 readiness, scored but not required for a passing result to weigh heavily
- **Fronting/inversion + epistemic modal production** (Item 23) — the closest this format gets to testing structures under self-generated, rather than recognised, conditions
