# C1 → C2 PLACEMENT TEST — Vocabulary Module (Day 31)

**Level:** C1 (Advanced) → C2 (Mastery/Proficient) | **Module:** Vocabulary | **AI Tutor:** Kak Ara

---

## Overview

This is the Day 31 assessment that closes the C1 Vocabulary track and determines whether a learner is ready for C2. It is built directly from the **format baku** already specified in `Materi/C1/Vocab.md` (`### H. Placement Test (Day 31)`, line 340), which deliberately departs from the multiple-choice format used at every level from A1 to B2.

**Why the format is different here, and not at any other level:** at A1–B2, grammatical accuracy is still being established, so a placement test built on discrete items (choose the right word, spot the error) is diagnostic. By the end of C1, accuracy in the twelve core C1 structures is assumed — the open question is no longer *"does this learner know the structure?"* but *"does the structure survive contact with a real argument, under real time pressure, and can the learner choose deliberately when to sound formal and when not to?"* That is not something a multiple-choice item can measure. It can only be observed in extended writing and unscripted speech — which is exactly what `Vocab.md`'s own format baku table specifies: **Writing (one formal argumentative essay, 150–200 words) + Speaking (three reflective/abstract questions)**, scored against register consistency and spontaneous fluency rather than a percentage.

> **Note on PRD consistency:** `PRD_USER.md` §4.2 states a generic Day 31 Vocabulary format (10–15 MCQ-style items, numeric score) that applies by default across all six levels. `Materi/C1/Vocab.md` overrides this default explicitly and specifically for C1→C2 with its own format baku table — consistent with the same file's broader claim that C1 is where the curriculum shifts from accuracy-testing to register/fluency-testing. This test follows the **C1-specific override**, since it is the more specific and more recently authored source for this exact deliverable. If this override was unintentional rather than a deliberate design decision, `PRD_USER.md` §4.2 should be updated to document the C1/C2 exception.

## Language Policy for This Test

Full English. No Indonesian, no translation, no Indonesian fallback — at any point, including instructions, scoring, and feedback. This matches `Vocab.md`'s own policy table (line 167–179): C1 is the level where the "Catatan Indonesia" column disappears entirely from correction tables, and no Indonesian clue is permitted even when a learner is stuck.

If the test-taker asks for a translation or an Indonesian clue, Kak Ara's scripted response is:

> "At C1 and C2 there's no Indonesian fallback — if something isn't landing, tell me which part, and I'll rephrase it in simpler English instead."

## What This Test Measures

| Dimension | What it checks | Why it matters at the C1→C2 boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Structural range | How many of the 12 core C1 structures appear, used correctly | Confirms the Day 1–30 grammar inventory is actually available, not just recognised |
| Structural naturalness | Whether those structures sound integrated into the argument, or feel inserted to satisfy a checklist | The line between "correct C1" and "stilted C1" — directly cited in `Vocab.md` line 20 as the actual B2→C1 differentiator, and the same logic extends one level up |
| Spontaneous fluency | Whether structures used confidently in the (planned) essay still appear in the (unplanned) speaking answers | `Vocab.md` line 20: at B2, complex structure shows up only with time to think; at C1, it should survive cognitive load. C2 requires this to be fully automatic |
| Deliberate register control | Whether the learner can switch formal ↔ natural register *on purpose*, not just default to one register throughout | Explicitly named as a C2-readiness criterion in `Vocab.md`'s own format-baku table, and is literally Day 12 of the C2 curriculum (Register-Shifting Fluency, formal↔colloquial↔slang) — this test checks for the first rung of that skill |
| Lexical naturalness | Whether Box-of-Words vocabulary (idiom, nominalisation, discourse marker) is deployed idiomatically, not just correctly | Distinguishes "knows the word" from "would actually say it this way" |

## Part 1 — Writing: Formal Argumentative Essay

**Instructions (shown to the test-taker, full English):**

> Choose **one** of the three prompts below. Write a formal, argumentative essay of **150–200 words** taking and defending a position. You don't need to mention every structure you've learned — five used naturally will tell us more than twelve used mechanically.
>
> **Required for a complete response:**
> - At least **5 of the 12 structures** listed below the prompts, deployed naturally
> - At least **5 words or phrases** from the Vocabulary Bank below
> - A consistent formal/academic register throughout — no informal slips

### Essay Topics (choose one)

**A.** Should social media platforms bear responsibility for amplifying misinformation, or does responsibility rest solely with those who create and share false content?

**B.** Is it ever justified to break a confidentiality agreement in the public interest?

**C.** Does automation threaten the dignity of work, or does it create the possibility of a more meaningful relationship with labour?

## Part 2 — Speaking: 3 Reflective/Abstract Questions

**Instructions (full English):** No preparation time. Answer as you would in real conversation — these questions are designed to test what survives without time to plan, not what you can construct carefully in writing.

**Q1 — Spontaneous counterfactual reflection:**
> "If one historical decision — any decision, from any country, any era — could be undone, which would you choose, and what do you think would have followed?"

*What this checks:* whether Day 1's inverted conditionals and counterfactual reasoning appear unprompted, under real-time pressure, not just in the planned essay.

**Q2 — Deliberate register switch (the core differentiator question):**
> "Explain the same idea twice, back to back: first the way you'd present it in a university seminar, then the way you'd explain it to a close friend over coffee. Topic: why people believe things that aren't true."

*What this checks:* the ability named explicitly in `Vocab.md`'s format-baku table — switching formal↔natural register *on purpose*, within a single response, rather than defaulting to one register throughout. This is the single most direct test of C2 readiness in this assessment.

**Q3 — Real-time abstract opinion, room for tone:**
> "Is there such a thing as 'the good old days'? Defend your answer."

*What this checks:* sustained spontaneous argumentation on an abstract prompt. The question is deliberately open enough to allow either a fully serious philosophical register or a self-aware, lightly ironic take — Kak Ara does not require wit or irony, but notes if it appears. (This question intentionally echoes C2 Day 28 — Memory, Nostalgia & the Language of "The Good Old Days" — as a forward-looking bridge, not a requirement.)

## Scoring & Interpretation

This test does not produce a percentage score — `Vocab.md`'s own format baku specifies qualitative criteria, because the C1→C2 boundary is about *how* language is used under pressure, not how many items are answered correctly.

| Band | Criteria |
|---|---|
| **Ready for C2** | Register stays formal/academic throughout the essay with no unintentional lapses. At least 5 of the 12 structures appear and sound integrated, not inserted. The structures used confidently in the essay also show up — in some form — in the spontaneous speaking answers. Q2 produces a genuine, deliberate register shift, not two versions of the same register. |
| **Almost ready** | Register is mostly consistent, with occasional unintentional informal slips. 3–4 structures appear, but one or two feel mechanically inserted rather than natural. Speaking answers fall back on simpler structures than the essay used — the structures are known but not yet automatic under pressure. Q2's register shift is attempted but only partially distinct. *(Name the specific structure(s) that most need reinforcing before retesting.)* |
| **Needs more C1 consolidation** | Frequent unintentional register slips, fewer than 3 structures present or used inaccurately, speaking answers rely on safe/simple sentence patterns throughout, and Q2 produces little or no audible difference between the two registers. *(Name the specific structure(s) and recommend specific Days to revisit.)* |

Kak Ara's feedback should always be **specific** — naming the actual structures observed or missing in this test-taker's own answers — never a generic template.

## Research Basis and Methodology

1. **Why essay + speaking, not multiple choice, is the correct format at this specific boundary.** `Vocab.md` itself documents (lines 17–20) that the B2→C1 differentiator was *spontaneous* structural accuracy under cognitive load, not accuracy with time to think. The same logic, one level up, makes a timed/untimed contrast the only valid way to test C1→C2: this test's Writing section (planned, time to construct) is deliberately paired against the Speaking section (unscripted) so the gap between them — not either section alone — is the real signal.

2. **What C2 specifically adds, drawn directly from `Materi/C2/Vocab.md`'s own 30-Day structure** (primary-source evidence, not external inference): the C2 curriculum's Grammar/Device list shows the C1→C2 shift is not "more grammar" but a shift in *kind* — from formal/academic accuracy (C1) to rhetorical and social control: Litotes & Understatement (C2 Day 2), Idiom Manipulation & Wordplay (Day 3), Hyperbole & Ironic Register-Shifting (Day 6), Register-Shifting Fluency formal↔colloquial↔slang (Day 12), Nuanced Near-Synonym & Connotation Precision (Day 4), and Neologism Awareness (Day 29) — none of which have any C1 equivalent, since C1's 12 structures are entirely formal/academic in function. This test's Speaking Q2 (deliberate register switch) and Q3 (room for tone) were designed as the first, smallest rung of exactly this ladder — not to require it, but to detect early readiness for it.

3. **Cambridge English Vocabulary Profile (EVP):** idiom inventories at sense level are documented at 39 idioms for C1 and 196 for C2 — roughly a five-fold increase, consistent with the C2 curriculum's heavy emphasis on idiom manipulation, wordplay, and native-like fixed expressions rather than simply "more vocabulary" of the same kind taught at C1.

4. **Vocabulary size research (Nation & Beglar; Milton):** C1 is generally placed at roughly 6,000–8,000 word families (Nation) for full text coverage; C2 is associated less with a higher raw count and more with depth of sense-knowledge per word (connotation, register-appropriateness, collocation precision) — which is why this test's rubric weights *naturalness* of vocabulary deployment over the *number* of distinct words used.

5. **Indonesian L1 learner consideration:** even at C1, near-synonym discrimination (choosing the precise word for tone, not just for meaning) remains a documented difficulty for Indonesian-L1 learners, since Indonesian frequently maps several English register-variants to a single native equivalent. This is one reason the Vocabulary Bank in Part 1 deliberately includes near-register-bound items (*notwithstanding the fact that* vs. plain *but*; *by virtue of* vs. plain *because*) rather than only topic-specific jargon — register precision, not topic vocabulary, is the C1-appropriate test.
