## Day 1 — Negotiating a Complex Deal

**Rationale:** BEC Higher C1 "operate confidently at senior levels"; GSE 67–75 "infer tone, recognise contrasting arguments, register-aware discourse." Core C1 negotiation — multi-stage, BATNA-aware, diplomatic under pressure.
**Register Focus:** C1 negotiation demands *diplomatic assertiveness* — firm on position, flexible on approach. Every phrase must manage face while protecting interests. Avoid both passive hedging and blunt assertion.

**Key Phrases:**
- *with all due respect* — respectful challenge opener; signals you're about to disagree without dismissing
- *I'd be inclined to* — hedged preference; softer than "I think", signals openness
- *that said* — pivoting concessive; acknowledges the other side then asserts your position
- *we may need to revisit* — diplomatic flag for unresolved issues; avoids "this is a problem"
- *on balance* — signals weighing process; executive-register conclusion marker
- *I take your point, but* — acknowledges the argument without conceding the position
- *where I'd differ is* — precise disagreement marker; avoids "you're wrong"
- *let's circle back to* — strategic deferral; keeps issue alive without blocking progress
- *that's a fair concern* — validates the other party's worry before addressing it
- *to play devil's advocate* — signals a challenge from an opposing angle, not personal attack
- *in the interest of* — outcome-framing; focuses both parties on shared goal
- *I'm not entirely convinced that* — hedged scepticism; executive-level doubt expression

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- move the needle -> "This concession should move the needle on the pricing discussion." (make meaningful progress)
- walk away from the table -> "Our BATNA is strong enough that we can walk away from the table." (exit a negotiation)
- split the difference -> "We could split the difference on the timeline." (compromise midway on a specific point)

**Challenge:** You have received a counter-offer that cuts your proposed margin by 30%. Write a professional reply (7–8 sentences): acknowledge their position, protect your core terms, propose a trade-off on a secondary point (payment timeline or delivery scope), and keep the deal moving forward.

**Speaking Q1:** How do you open a complex negotiation in a way that signals both confidence and genuine openness to the other party?
**Speaking Q2:** What strategies do you use when a negotiation reaches an impasse and neither side is willing to move?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you balance assertiveness and diplomacy when the other party pushes back hard on your most important terms?

## Day 2 — Crisis Communication (Internal)

**Rationale:** GSE 67–75 register-aware discourse; BEC Higher C1 "senior level communication under pressure." Internal crisis comms requires managing uncertainty, maintaining authority, and preventing information vacuums.
**Register Focus:** Crisis register must be *calm and authoritative without false reassurance*. Precision on what is known vs unknown is itself a trust signal. Avoid both panic-inducing language and dismissive minimisation.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"I'm not entirely convinced that"* (Day 1) — when would a crisis update need to diplomatically challenge an assumption being circulated?

**Key Phrases:**
- *we want to keep everyone informed* — trust signal; transparent intent from the outset
- *the situation is developing rapidly* — honest pace-setter; avoids false certainty
- *at this stage, what we know is* — precision framing; separates confirmed from speculation
- *our immediate priority is* — action-focus signal; prevents circular discussion
- *we are taking the following steps* — structured credibility; shows a plan exists
- *I want to be upfront about what we don't yet know* — psychological safety; normalises uncertainty
- *we are committed to providing regular updates* — trust maintenance; establishes expectations
- *please direct all questions to* — information management; prevents rumour spread
- *I want to reassure you that* — calibrated tone; use only with genuine warrant
- *our contingency plan is* — executive competence signal; shows foresight not just reaction
- *this is being handled at the highest level* — authority signal; de-escalates without dismissing
- *rest assured we are on top of this* — closing confidence marker; only use when warranted

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- get ahead of this -> "We need to get ahead of this before the story breaks externally." (act before a problem escalates)
- steady the ship -> "Our job right now is to steady the ship while we assess the damage." (stabilise a situation)
- hold the line -> "The team needs to hold the line on external comms until we're aligned." (maintain a position under pressure)

**Challenge:** A critical system failure has made your platform unavailable to all clients for six hours. Write an internal memo (8–9 sentences): acknowledge the situation clearly, share what is confirmed, explain your immediate response steps, set expectations for the next update, and maintain a calm authoritative tone throughout.

**Speaking Q1:** What are the most important principles to keep in mind when communicating with employees during a genuine organisational crisis?
**Speaking Q2:** How do you balance being transparent with employees while managing information that may be legally or competitively sensitive?
**Speaking Q3:** What are the most common mistakes leaders make in internal crisis communication, and how do you avoid them?

## Day 3 — Delivering Bad News Diplomatically

**Rationale:** Brown & Levinson politeness theory — bad news delivery = face-threatening act requiring maximal negative politeness; BEC Higher C1 "communicate difficult messages at senior level." C1 demands controlled empathy with structural clarity.
**Register Focus:** Avoid the two failure modes: cold efficiency (no emotional acknowledgement) and excessive softening (message is obscured). The news must land clearly while the delivery remains considerate.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"Our immediate priority is"* (Day 2) — how does a bad news message need to immediately redirect priorities for the recipient?

**Key Phrases:**
- *I appreciate your continued commitment to this* — opens with genuine acknowledgement of the other party's investment
- *unfortunately, I have to share some challenging news* — honest framing; avoids burying the lede
- *I want to be transparent with you about* — trust signal before a difficult disclosure
- *after careful consideration, we have decided to* — signals deliberate process, not arbitrary action
- *I understand this may be disappointing to hear* — direct empathy without over-emoting
- *this was not a decision we took lightly* — weight-of-decision marker; signals it mattered
- *going forward, what this means for you is* — practical clarity after the emotional acknowledgement
- *we want to support you through this transition* — future-orientation; moves from problem to path forward
- *I'm happy to discuss this further* — accessibility signal; prevents the conversation feeling closed
- *I'd welcome your thoughts on how we move forward* — invites agency back to the other party
- *we believe this is the right decision for* — brief rationale; not a defensive justification
- *I'm confident we can navigate this together* — forward-focus closing; only use if genuinely warranted

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- soften the blow -> "The way you structured the message helped soften the blow considerably." (reduce impact of difficult news)
- come to terms with -> "Give them time to come to terms with the decision before expecting a response." (gradually accept something difficult)
- put this behind us -> "Once we've processed this, I'd like to put this behind us and focus forward." (move past a difficult event)

**Challenge:** Your company must withdraw from a major contract commitment with a long-standing client due to a strategic shift. Write an email (8–9 sentences): acknowledge the impact clearly, provide brief honest reasoning, outline what happens next for the client's transition, and leave the relationship intact.

**Speaking Q1:** How do you structure a difficult message so the core information is clear without being brutal or overly softened?
**Speaking Q2:** How do you handle a situation where the person receiving bad news becomes visibly upset or angry in the moment?
**Speaking Q3:** When is it appropriate to deliver significant bad news in writing versus in person, and what changes between the two formats?

## Day 4 — Giving Critical Feedback to a Peer

**Rationale:** Brown & Levinson — peer feedback = face-threatening act; requires positive politeness to preserve relationship + negative politeness to communicate clearly. BEC Higher C1 "communicate difficult messages diplomatically." C1 peers require *specific, evidence-based, non-hierarchical* critique.
**Register Focus:** Peer feedback at C1 avoids both the corporate euphemism ("you've been amazing but...") and the blunt challenge. The register should be *collegial-direct*: honest, evidence-anchored, and solution-focused. The relationship must survive the feedback.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"I want to be transparent with you about"* (Day 3) — how would you use this to open a difficult peer feedback conversation?

**Key Phrases:**
- *I want to share some observations that I think could be useful* — intent-first opener; frames feedback as gift not attack
- *I've noticed a pattern that I'd like to discuss* — evidence-based (pattern, not single incident); invites discussion
- *my concern is the impact this is having on* — shifts to consequences, not personality
- *I want to be direct with you because I respect your work* — honesty as compliment; builds credibility
- *could we explore why this keeps happening?* — curiosity over blame; opens diagnostic conversation
- *what I'd like to see going forward is* — concrete expectation; moves from diagnosis to direction
- *I think you're capable of more than this shows* — growth signal; separates the person from the behaviour
- *this isn't about blame — it's about finding a way forward* — reframes the conversation as collaborative
- *I'd value your perspective on what's driving this* — genuine inquiry; not rhetorical
- *my suggestion would be to* — advisory not directive; appropriate for peer-to-peer
- *I'd like us to agree on a clear path forward* — closes with commitment; prevents conversation remaining abstract
- *what support do you need from me on this?* — signals partnership, not judgment

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- call it out -> "I wanted to call it out directly rather than let it continue affecting the team." (address a problem explicitly)
- have each other's backs -> "Part of a strong peer relationship is having each other's backs — and that includes honest feedback." (support and protect each other)
- course-correct -> "It's much easier to course-correct now than after the next milestone review." (make adjustments before a problem worsens)

**Challenge:** A senior peer colleague's persistent lack of follow-through has started affecting your team's deliverables. Write a structured peer feedback message (8–9 sentences): be specific about the pattern with evidence, communicate the impact, make a concrete suggestion, and preserve the working relationship.

**Speaking Q1:** What is the difference between giving feedback to a peer versus giving it to someone you manage, and how does that change your approach?
**Speaking Q2:** How do you handle a peer who becomes defensive or dismissive when you give them honest feedback?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you decide when a peer performance issue should be resolved between the two of you versus escalated to a manager?

## Day 5 — Leading a Strategy Meeting

**Rationale:** CEFR CV C1 Spoken Interaction — "can chair a discussion effectively, enforce procedural rules"; BEC Higher C1 "lead meetings at senior level." Strategy meetings require a different facilitation register from operational meetings: broader questions, longer time horizons, tolerating productive ambiguity.
**Register Focus:** Strategy facilitation at C1 requires *intellectual authority without dominance*. The leader must open up the thinking space, not close it. Language that signals genuine curiosity and systemic thinking — not meeting management mechanics.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"What I'd like to see going forward is"* (Day 4) — how would you use this to establish a strategic expectation at the start of a planning meeting?

**Key Phrases:**
- *let's take a step back and look at the bigger picture* — signals strategic frame, not operational problem-solving
- *I want to make sure we're challenging our assumptions here* — intellectual permission-giving; invites challenge
- *what are we not seeing that we should be?* — structured blind-spot inquiry; classic strategic facilitation move
- *before we commit to this direction, let's pressure-test it* — risk-acknowledgement before commitment
- *I'd like to open this up — who sees it differently?* — explicit invitation for dissenting views
- *the question we really need to answer is* — reframes the discussion toward the strategic core
- *let me reframe this slightly* — gentle course-correction without dismissing the previous contribution
- *the strategic implication of this is* — elevates a tactical observation to systemic consequence
- *we're in danger of solving the wrong problem if* — warning flag for misaligned problem-definition
- *what would have to be true for this to work?* — pre-mortem framing; surfaces assumptions that need testing
- *I want to make sure dissenting views are heard* — psychological safety signal; explicitly protects minority views
- *let's stress-test this scenario* — analytical rigour signal; elevates discussion quality

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- zoom out -> "Before we go deeper on this, can we zoom out and look at the pattern across all three quarters?" (take a broader perspective)
- cut through the noise -> "What I'd like this session to do is help us cut through the noise and identify the two or three things that really matter." (focus on what is most important)
- devil in the details -> "The strategy is solid — the devil is in the details of implementation." (the difficult part is in the specifics)

**Challenge:** Write a strategy meeting agenda (5–6 agenda items with time allocation) and an opening statement (4–5 sentences) for a quarterly planning session of senior leaders. The opening should: establish the strategic frame, signal how decisions will be made, and make clear you want all perspectives — including dissenting ones — on the table.

**Speaking Q1:** How do you distinguish between a strategy meeting and a planning meeting, and what does that distinction mean for how you facilitate?
**Speaking Q2:** What do you do when a strategy meeting gets stuck in operational detail and loses its strategic frame?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you handle a dominant voice in a strategy meeting that consistently shuts down alternative thinking?

## Day 6 — Presenting to Senior Leadership

**Rationale:** BEC Higher C1 "present complex information clearly and concisely to senior audiences"; GSE 67–75 "address difficult topics persuasively." Executive presentations require radical concision — saying the most important thing, once, clearly.
**Register Focus:** Senior leadership register = *executive concision + confident framing*. No warm-up, no hedging on key recommendations, no burying the ask. Structure: headline first, reasoning after. The leader's time is the constraint; every sentence must earn its place.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"We're in danger of solving the wrong problem if"* (Day 5) — how would you use this to reframe a strategic concern in an executive briefing?

**Key Phrases:**
- *I'll keep this concise and to the point* — respects time; sets executive-appropriate register from the start
- *the bottom line is* — announces the headline; prevents burying the key message
- *the three things I need from you today are* — asks first (executive preference); clarity on the purpose of the meeting
- *the data points to a clear direction here* — confidence signal; avoids unnecessary uncertainty hedging
- *I'd like to anticipate the question you may have about* — pre-empts objections; signals preparation
- *my recommendation is, and here's the logic behind it* — recommendation before rationale (not the other way around)
- *the risk of inaction here is* — frames urgency; makes the cost of not deciding visible
- *I've triangulated this from multiple sources* — credibility signal; reduces perceived bias of single-source claim
- *I want to be direct about the trade-offs* — honesty signal; builds trust with executives who hate surprises
- *what this means for your priorities is* — connects the recommendation to the leader's existing agenda
- *I'm happy to go deeper on any of these points* — offers depth without imposing it
- *the decision I need from you is* — clean ask; makes the action required explicit

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- cut to the chase -> "I'll cut to the chase — our recommendation is to exit this market by Q3." (get directly to the most important point)
- put it on the table -> "I want to put all the options on the table before we decide." (make everything visible before choosing)
- the elephant in the room -> "I want to address the elephant in the room — our margins have been declining for three consecutive quarters." (acknowledge an obvious problem being avoided)

**Challenge:** Write the first two minutes of an executive briefing (7–8 sentences) proposing a significant investment to a senior leadership team. Hook with the business problem, state your recommendation clearly, acknowledge the primary risk, and close with a specific decision request. No warm-up, no excessive context.

**Speaking Q1:** What specifically changes about your communication style when you move from presenting to peers to presenting to senior leadership?
**Speaking Q2:** How do you handle a difficult question in an executive presentation where you don't have a confident answer yet?
**Speaking Q3:** What are the most common mistakes professionals make when presenting to senior leadership, and how do you avoid them?

## Day 7 — Defending a Proposal Under Scrutiny

**Rationale:** GSE 67–75 "recognise contrasting arguments, produce persuasive argument"; BEC Higher C1 "defend a position under questioning." C1 requires holding a position under pressure while remaining intellectually honest about genuine uncertainties.
**Register Focus:** Proposal defence register at C1 = *confident, evidence-anchored, non-defensive*. The register must distinguish between conceding a point (good — intellectual honesty) and conceding the position (different — give ground strategically, not under social pressure). Avoid argumentativeness; maintain analytical posture.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"My recommendation is, and here's the logic behind it"* (Day 6) — when defending under scrutiny, how would you return to this framing after addressing an objection?

**Key Phrases:**
- *that's a valid concern — let me address it directly* — validates before defending; non-defensive opener
- *I anticipated that question, and here's the data* — preparation signal; shows robustness not improvisation
- *let me be clear about what this proposal does and doesn't claim* — scope clarity; reduces over-reading of the claim
- *I'd push back on that interpretation slightly* — precise, respectful disagreement with an interpretation
- *the evidence for this position is* — anchors defence in data, not conviction alone
- *I want to acknowledge where the uncertainty lies* — intellectual honesty; builds credibility with sceptics
- *I've stress-tested this against the alternative scenarios* — rigour signal; shows the proposal is not fragile
- *that's a fair challenge, and it led me to modify the approach* — concession as credibility; shows responsiveness
- *on this specific point, I'm confident because* — confident but scoped; not blanket confidence
- *even if we accept your assumption, the conclusion still holds* — logical robustness; handles adversarial inputs
- *I'd invite you to look at this differently* — reframing offer; not dismissing the objection
- *I'm open to interrogating this further — which part concerns you most?* — invites focused challenge; de-broadens attacks

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- hold up under scrutiny -> "I'm confident this proposal will hold up under scrutiny when you look at the underlying data." (remain strong when examined closely)
- poke holes in -> "I'd actually welcome the chance to poke holes in this before we present it to the board." (critically examine to find weaknesses)
- stand behind -> "I stand behind this analysis, and I'm happy to walk through the methodology step by step." (maintain confidence in something)

**Challenge:** A key stakeholder has submitted a detailed three-point critique of your proposal (cost overrun, poor timing, uncertain ROI). Write a rebuttal (8–9 sentences): address each concern with specific evidence or reasoning, acknowledge one genuine uncertainty honestly, and close by moving the conversation forward without conceding the core recommendation.

**Speaking Q1:** What is the difference between defending a proposal and being defensive about it, and how do you stay on the right side of that line?
**Speaking Q2:** How do you respond when a senior person challenges your proposal with what you know to be a flawed assumption?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you decide in the moment which objections to concede and which to hold firm on?

## Day 8 — Mediating a Team Conflict

**Rationale:** CEFR CV C1 Mediation — "able to act as mediator in formal and informal discussions"; GSE 67–75 "facilitate discussion, manage interactions." Team conflict mediation at C1 requires third-party neutrality — not arbitration (deciding who is right) but facilitation (helping both find a workable path).
**Register Focus:** Mediator register at C1 = *neutral, curious, process-visible*. Avoid siding, interpreting too quickly, or problem-solving prematurely. The mediator's language makes the process transparent and creates safety for both parties to contribute genuinely.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"Even if we accept your assumption, the conclusion still holds"* (Day 7) — how would a mediator use this kind of logical framing to help two parties find common ground?

**Key Phrases:**
- *I want to create a space where both perspectives are heard* — opening intent-signal; establishes psychological safety
- *let me make sure I understand your position correctly* — reflective listening; prevents misrepresentation
- *I'm hearing two different priorities here — and both are valid* — acknowledges both; avoids implying one is wrong
- *I want to separate the issue from the people involved* — depersonalisation frame; classic mediation move
- *what would a workable solution look like for you?* — future-orientation; shifts from grievance to resolution
- *let's look for common ground before focusing on differences* — convergence-first approach; builds momentum
- *I want to check — am I representing your view accurately?* — consent loop; keeps mediation honest
- *the goal here is to find a path forward we can both commit to* — shared-outcome framing; prevents win-lose dynamic
- *I'd like to try reframing this slightly* — process-visible pivot; signals a deliberate facilitation move
- *where do you feel the other perspective has merit?* — perspective-taking prompt; reduces entrenched positions
- *let's agree on what we're actually trying to achieve* — goal-reset; useful when the argument has drifted from the issue
- *I'm going to hold off on taking sides until I hear more* — neutrality signal; removes early pressure to judge

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- bridge the gap -> "Part of my role here is to help you bridge the gap between your two positions." (connect two different views)
- at an impasse -> "It seems like we're at an impasse — let me try a different approach." (when both sides are stuck)
- meet in the middle -> "I don't think you're as far apart as it feels right now — there's a real possibility to meet in the middle." (find a compromise)

**Challenge:** Two senior team members are in dispute over ownership of a cross-functional project. The conflict has become visible to the wider team. Write a facilitation plan (5–6 steps with key phrases for each) and an opening script (4–5 sentences) for a mediation session: establish ground rules, set the tone, and create the conditions for both to feel genuinely heard.

**Speaking Q1:** How do you maintain genuine neutrality when mediating a conflict where you privately think one person is more at fault?
**Speaking Q2:** What do you do when a mediation session breaks down and one or both parties refuse to engage constructively?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you know when a team conflict requires formal mediation versus informal intervention by a manager?

## Day 9 — Writing a Diplomatic Refusal

**Rationale:** Brown & Levinson negative politeness — refusal = face-threatening act; C1 must manage face for both parties while being unambiguous. BEC Higher C1 genre: business refusal letters; Bhatia move analysis (acknowledge, decline, reason, alternative, maintain relationship).
**Register Focus:** Diplomatic refusal at C1 must be *clear, honest, and relationship-preserving simultaneously*. The refusal must be unambiguous (no "maybe later" masking a no), the reasoning brief (not defensive), and the forward-bridge genuine (not formulaic). Every word should make the recipient feel respected even through the disappointment.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"The goal here is to find a path forward we can both commit to"* (Day 8) — even in a refusal, how might you still use this framing to offer something constructive?

**Key Phrases:**
- *thank you for thinking of us for this* — genuine acknowledgement of the other party's initiative
- *I've given this careful thought, and I have to be honest with you* — signals considered decision, not reflexive rejection
- *on this occasion, we're not in a position to* — precise, non-permanent framing; avoids "never"
- *I want to be transparent about the reasons behind this* — honesty signal; selective but genuine
- *I hope this doesn't close the door on future opportunities* — relationship bridge; only if genuinely meant
- *this isn't a reflection of our confidence in you* — separates the refusal from the relationship value
- *I genuinely appreciate the thought that went into this proposal* — specific praise before the decline
- *rather than leave you without options, I'd suggest* — alternative offer; makes the refusal constructive
- *I want to maintain the quality of our relationship going forward* — explicit relationship priority signal
- *I hope you can understand the position we're in* — appeals to mutual understanding without over-explaining
- *I remain committed to finding ways to collaborate where it makes sense* — scope-calibrated openness; not a blanket future promise
- *please don't hesitate to approach us again* — genuine invitation; closes the loop positively

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- close the door on -> "I want to decline this without closing the door on our longer-term partnership." (end this but leave future options open)
- leave the door open -> "Declining now doesn't mean we're leaving the door closed — this may look different in six months." (keep future possibilities alive)
- not the right fit -> "At this stage, this isn't the right fit for our portfolio, but I wanted to be honest about that rather than delay." (mismatch without personal rejection)

**Challenge:** A long-term partner has invited your company to co-invest in a project that doesn't align with your current strategic priorities. Write a diplomatic refusal email (8–9 sentences): decline clearly without ambiguity, explain your constraint briefly without being defensive, offer an alternative form of support or engagement, and protect the relationship.

**Speaking Q1:** How do you make a refusal feel respectful and final at the same time, without leaving the other person hanging or confused?
**Speaking Q2:** What is the right level of explanation to include in a business refusal, and when does too much explanation become counterproductive?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you handle a situation where the person receiving a refusal keeps pushing back and won't accept the answer?

## Day 10 — Cross-Cultural Negotiation

**Rationale:** Bhatia genre analysis + cross-cultural pragmatics; GSE 67–75 "infer tone and cultural register"; BEC Higher C1 "navigate culturally complex business situations." C1 requires *active cultural meta-awareness* — not just sensitivity, but explicit calibration of process and register for the specific cultural context.
**Register Focus:** Cross-cultural negotiation at C1 demands *process transparency + invitation for correction*. Don't assume shared norms. High-context cultures (Japan, Gulf states, Korea) prioritise relationship before transaction; pace, silence, and indirection are information. Naming the process ("can I ask how this typically works?") is itself a sophisticated move.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"I remain committed to finding ways to collaborate where it makes sense"* (Day 9) — how would you adapt this phrase for a high-context cultural context where directness is less valued?

**Key Phrases:**
- *I want to make sure we're approaching this in a way that works for everyone* — process-collaboration signal from the outset
- *I'm conscious that our practices may differ here* — explicit meta-awareness; names the cultural variable
- *I'd like to understand how this typically works on your side before we proceed* — learning posture; respects their norms as primary
- *I don't want to make assumptions about your expectations* — anti-presumption signal; humility as strategy
- *culturally, we tend to approach this as* — self-disclosure of your own norms; creates reciprocal transparency
- *please feel free to flag if anything I'm doing isn't reading well* — permission to correct; extremely high-trust signal
- *I want to be sure I'm not inadvertently causing offence* — explicit intent; protects relational face
- *can I ask — is this the usual approach in your context?* — genuine cultural inquiry; non-patronising
- *I want to find an approach that feels right for both of us* — mutual framing; not "we'll do it your way"
- *the pace of this discussion — is it working for everyone?* — calibration check; pace is cultural
- *I appreciate your patience as we navigate this together* — relational acknowledgement during uncertainty
- *I'd value your candid assessment* — explicit request for honest feedback; invites cultural correction

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
- read the room -> "You have to read the room in cross-cultural settings — what works in New York won't land the same way in Tokyo." (understand and respond to the atmosphere)
- lose face -> "In this context, publicly disagreeing could cause them to lose face — better to raise concerns privately." (lose social standing or dignity)
- on the same page -> "Before we go further, I want to make sure we're on the same page about the process, not just the content." (have a shared understanding)

**Challenge:** You are about to enter a first-round negotiation with a potential strategic partner based in a high-context culture (your choice of context). Write a pre-negotiation email (8–9 sentences): establish the relationship warmly, signal your respect for their process, propose a first meeting without being transactional, and gently set expectations for your own side's communication style.

**Speaking Q1:** How do you adapt your negotiation approach when the cultural context values relationship-building over reaching agreement quickly?
**Speaking Q2:** What specific signals — verbal or non-verbal — do you look for to understand whether a cross-cultural negotiation is going well?
**Speaking Q3:** How do you respond professionally when you realise you have unintentionally offended a counterpart from a different cultural background?

## Day 11 — Managing Up (Communicating With Bosses)

**Rationale:** GSE 67–75 register-aware professional discourse; BEC Higher C1 "communicate effectively with senior stakeholders." Managing up at C1 requires a specific register: *proactive, solution-oriented, calibrated on what the executive needs to know vs what you can handle independently*.
**Register Focus:** Managing-up register must avoid two failure modes: (1) over-escalating (losing executive confidence), (2) under-informing (creating surprises). The register signals *I have this under control, but I need you on this specific point*. Visibility without alarm; precision without overload.

**Frasa Kunci Refresh:** *"I'd value your candid assessment"* (Day 10) — how would you use this phrase appropriately when seeking your manager's view on a situation you are navigating?

**Key Phrases:**
- *I wanted to make sure you have visibility on this before it escalates* — proactive flag; prevents surprise
- *I'd value your guidance on how to handle this* — specific ask (guidance not rescue); signals you're handling it
- *my recommendation is X — I'm bringing it to you because I'd like your backing* — solution-first, then the ask
- *I want to be upfront about a challenge I'm navigating* — transparency signal; manages expectations
- *I've already taken these steps, and here's where I need your help* — shows action taken before escalating
- *I don't want to take this further without your awareness* — appropriate boundary-signalling
- *I'm conscious this has implications beyond my remit* — self-aware scope identification; not overstepping
- *what's your read on how we should position this?* — executive perspective-seeking; values their strategic view
- *I want to give you an accurate picture — including the parts that are uncertain* — honest briefing; not spin
- *I'll own the outcome on this — I just need a decision from you on* — accountability + specific ask
- *before I proceed, I wanted to sense-check with you* — judgment-check; shows good judgment by checking
- *I'll keep you posted unless you tell me otherwise* — appropriate closure; gives executive control of cadence

**Frasa Siap Pakai:**
